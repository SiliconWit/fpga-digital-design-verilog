The same Verilog that configures an FPGA can also become a custom chip. Until recently, seeing that process required an expensive licence and a non-disclosure agreement. Open tools changed that. In this capstone you take a design you already built and run it through the real steps a chip goes through on its way to a foundry, and you finish with a layout you can view. #asic #sky130 #openlane

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. **Contrast** an FPGA with an ASIC and explain when each makes sense.
2. **Describe** the ASIC flow: synthesis, floorplan, placement, clock tree, routing, and signoff.
3. **Run** OpenLane on the Sky130 PDK to take RTL to GDSII.
4. **Open** and interpret the resulting layout at a basic level.

:::tip[Instrument it]
Signoff is itself a measurement discipline: the flow reports timing slack, area, and design-rule violations, and a clean tape-out means reading those numbers honestly rather than forcing the design through. Treat the run summary as your instrument, watch the worst negative slack and the DRC count the way you would watch a sensor reading, and tighten the design until they are in spec. The same mindset of [making a system report its own health](https://siliconwit.io) applies whether the system is a deployed sensor or a chip on its way to a foundry.
:::

## Application Questions and Solutions

### Question 1: FPGA prototype or straight to ASIC?

A team has a working design on an FPGA and expects to ship 5,000 units. A colleague argues they should tape out an ASIC immediately to cut unit cost. What is the flaw in that reasoning?

<details>
<summary>**Click to reveal the solution**</summary>

<Steps>

1. **Weigh the non-recurring cost.** An ASIC tape-out carries large one-time mask and engineering costs that only pay off at high volume. ✅

2. **Compare against volume.** Spread across 5,000 units, those one-time costs usually dwarf the per-unit saving, so an FPGA or a low-cost MCU is often cheaper overall at this scale. ✅

3. **Consider risk and time.** An FPGA can be reprogrammed after a bug, a manufactured ASIC cannot. At modest volumes the flexibility and lower risk of the FPGA typically win. ✅

</Steps>

</details>
